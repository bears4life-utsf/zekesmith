# Story Brief: When SAFe Stops Scaling

**Publication:** Building Better Software  
**Working title:** When SAFe Stops Scaling  
**Subtitle:** Lessons from leading a product organization through the transition from centralized planning to empowered product teams  
**Document type:** Story Brief (pre-writing)  
**Date:** July 23, 2026  
**Source of truth:** Interview Preparation Dossier (July 23, 2026)  
**Editor role:** Editor-in-Chief — narrative selection only. No article draft. No outline-as-table-of-contents.

---

## Editorial Stance

This is not an anti-SAFe essay.

If a reader finishes and thinks “SAFe bad, POM good,” we failed.

If a reader finishes and thinks “Every operating model has a useful life — and leaders are responsible for noticing when its strengths have become constraints,” we succeeded.

The dossier’s strongest intellectual asset is not the before/after metrics. It is the **second-act honesty**: ownership improved, ceremony decreased, throughput held — and then “safe-lite” reappeared, customer instrumentation lagged, and dependency pain returned without SAFe’s scaffolding. That incomplete arc is what makes the piece durable. Controversy ages. Incomplete transformation does not.

---

## 1. Central Thesis

**Operating models do not fail because they are wrong. They stop scaling when the problems they were designed to solve give way to problems their structure now creates — and leadership keeps optimizing the old solution.**

Alternate one-liner (slightly sharper, still balanced):

**The danger is not adopting a scaling framework. The danger is mistaking a framework that once created clarity for a permanent answer to how work should be owned.**

If a reader remembers one idea six months later, it should be the first sentence.

---

## 2. Reader Transformation

### Before reading

Experienced leaders typically hold one of these beliefs:

- “SAFe is the problem” (or “SAFe is the solution”) — a binary framed around a brand name.
- “If we change the ceremonies, the culture will follow.”
- “Scaling frameworks are either mature enterprise practice or consultant theater.”
- “Empowered teams are obviously better; anyone still using heavy planning is behind.”
- Or the opposite: “Without centralized planning, large organizations dissolve into chaos.”

### After finishing

The reader should believe:

- Operating models are temporary answers to a specific class of organizational problems.
- Centralized planning can be rational — even necessary — at one stage of scale.
- The warning sign is not “too many meetings.” It is **ownership migrating away from the people closest to the customer problem**, while dashboards continue to look healthy.
- Leaving a heavyweight model without replacing coordination, discovery, and outcome habits creates a vacuum — and vacuums refill with the old power structure under a new label.
- The real leadership job is not picking the fashionable model. It is noticing when the model’s strengths have become the organization’s constraints.

Emotional residue: recognition, not triumph. “I’ve seen this.” Not “I was right to hate SAFe.”

## 3. The Three Big Ideas

Limit the article to these three. Everything else supports them or belongs in the companion essay.

### Idea 1 — Useful until it isn’t

SAFe (and frameworks like it) often succeed first because they create shared cadence, dependency visibility, and planning discipline across a complex portfolio. That success is real. The essay must say so plainly — including voices who still miss dependency mapping.

**Why memorable:** It disarms the SAFe-defender and the SAFe-critic in the same paragraph.

### Idea 2 — The model starts optimizing itself

The failure mode is not “agile theater” as a slogan. It is specific: PI planning that behaves like a mini-waterfall; commitments that outrun discovery; ownership split between PMO coordination and team delivery; tools so complex they encode the operating model’s confusion (nearly 40 Jira statuses); dashboards that diverge from unplanned reality.

**Why memorable:** Concrete, recognizable, non-ideological. Leaders have sat in that room.

### Idea 3 — Changing the label is not changing the system

The most important finding in the dossier is not the epic/story lift. It is **safe-lite**: centralized decision conduits returning after the ceremonies left — and the survey truth that teams gained accountability faster than they gained customer instrumentation, success measures, and dependency habits.

**Why memorable:** It is the twist. Readers expect “we left SAFe and things got better.” The lasting insight is “we left SAFe and discovered which problems were the framework — and which were us.”

## 4. Narrative Arc

Structure as a story with Acts — not as a white paper with sections titled “Background / Findings / Recommendations.”

### Act I — Why it made sense

**Purpose:** Establish intellectual honesty. Show the reader you understand why a large, multi-product organization adopts a scaling framework.

**Emotional tone:** Respectful, grounded, slightly nostalgic for the clarity it once provided. No sneering.

**Supporting evidence:**
- 2022 SAFe training/implementation seriousness (ARTs, PI planning, program boards, ROAM, Inspect & Adapt) — this was not casual.
- Stated structural need: many products, many teams, many dependencies, need for shared cadence and release coordination.
- Cortney Dreiske (2026): dependency mapping/scheduling was “one of the (few) great things we had with SAFe.”
- Survey comment that project managers once did heavy coordination so teams could focus.

**Transition into Act II:**  
“The question was never whether planning had value. The question became what kind of planning — and who owned the decisions that planning was supposed to serve.”

### Act II — The quiet inversion

**Purpose:** Show the turning point without melodrama. The model that created visibility begins creating rigidity; process begins managing the product rather than the reverse.

**Emotional tone:** Unease. Recognition. The slow realization in leadership conversations, not a single cinematic rant.

**Supporting evidence:**
- March 2023 Program CI: PI1/PI2 as “a mini-waterfall wrapper on Agile”; fixed scope + fixed timelines; difficulty changing priority inside the increment.
- July/August 2024 Balaji review: late SME involvement; dependencies found after backlog entry; missing E2E ownership; PI dashboards that didn’t match unplanned work; features stuck for one incomplete story.
- Beth Goodman’s lived PM frustration: two-day PI planning; barriers to talking with developers before stories.
- Tooling as symptom: Xactimate Desktop with nearly 40 Jira statuses; XWCLMS ownership murk; fragmented projects.
- Your own private candor (use carefully, anonymize colleagues): excitement to leave SAFe — human, not performative.

**Transition into Act III:**  
“By the time people asked whether PI planning would continue next year, the organization was no longer debating a framework. It was debating who was allowed to own a customer problem.”

### Act III — The decision and the first results

**Purpose:** Show leadership action as structural (ownership, product groups, discovery/delivery together) — not as “we cancelled a ceremony.”

**Emotional tone:** Determined, practical, still humble about proof.

**Supporting evidence:**
- October 2024 restructure announcements: product domains, POM principles, product-team accountability.
- Explicit goal framing: break even with SAFe first (intellectually honest bar).
- March 2025 Estimate Pillar comparison: epics/stories up; independent/ad hoc stories down — interpreted carefully as throughput and focus signals, not proof of customer value.
- September 2025: clearer ownership; many SAFe issues resolved at team level; next problem is cross-team objective alignment.

**Transition into Act IV:**  
“The first layer of the transition worked well enough to be dangerous — because early success can convince leaders the hard part is over.”

### Act IV — The incomplete middle (the essay’s lasting contribution)

**Purpose:** This is the Act most essays skip. Include it. It is why the piece will still matter in five years.

**Emotional tone:** Honest, slightly chastened, useful. No victory lap.

**Supporting evidence:**
- “Safe-lite” (Sept 2025): old centralization under a new name; .NET 8 as concrete example of bottlenecked decision-making.
- April 2026 X1 note: primary communication/decision conduit limiting Product/Engineering collaboration.
- POM survey (168 responses): highs in accountability/empowerment; lows in customer data access, instrumentation, success measures before delivery, iterating/sunsetting, cross-pillar dependencies.
- Cortney’s reminder that dependency mapping was a SAFe strength — and survey scores showing dependency management still weak.

**Closing beat of the Act (not the essay ending yet):**  
The organization had moved past ceremony faster than it had matured into outcome management. That is not failure. It is the real work.

### Coda — What leaders should watch for

**Purpose:** Convert the story into a transferable lens — without becoming a checklist article.

**Emotional tone:** Quiet authority. Peer to peer.

**Content:** Warning signs that a scaling model has stopped scaling (ownership diffusion, dashboard theater, discovery separated from delivery, coordination as the product, local optimization). End on evolution, not condemnation.

## 5. Opening

### Option A — The question in the room

Open on Devin Haueter’s September 2024 question: *Will PI planning continue in 2025?*  
Not dramatic. Ordinary. The kind of question that only gets asked when the operating model is already dead in people’s minds.

**Strength:** Instant recognition for anyone who has lived a transition. Low rhetoric.

### Option B — Mini-waterfall (recommended)

Open on the March 2023 continuous-improvement line: PI planning had begun to feel like “a mini-waterfall wrapper on Agile.”  
Then: that sentence was written while the organization was still operating inside SAFe — not by an outsider criticizing it, but by people trying to improve it.

**Strength:** Establishes balance immediately. The critique begins as self-scrutiny, not branding warfare. Timeless. Specific. Evidence-bound.

### Option C — The 40 statuses

Open on a single operational absurdity: a desktop product workflow with nearly forty Jira statuses.  
Then: tools rarely invent complexity; they archive it.

**Strength:** Visceral and memorable. Risk: can feel like a cheap shot at process if not quickly widened to ownership and decision rights.

### Editorial recommendation

**Lead with Option B (mini-waterfall).**  
Within the first screen, acknowledge why SAFe was adopted and what it did well (cadence, dependency visibility). That sequence — *we saw the problem from inside; we also know what the model solved* — is the HBR/Stripe register. Option A can appear later as the social proof that the organization had already moved on emotionally. Option C belongs in Act II as evidence, not as the thesis carrier.

## 6. Ending

### Ending A — The safe-lite mirror (recommended)

End on the idea that leaving a framework is easier than leaving the habits that made the framework feel necessary.  
“Safe-lite” becomes the final image: when decision rights reconsolidate, the organization has changed its vocabulary more than its operating system.

**Why it works:** Leaves the reader examining their own org. Not a summary. Not a CTA. A diagnostic.

### Ending B — The dependency paradox

End on Cortney’s point and the survey’s dependency scores together: you can remove the ceremony that made dependencies visible and still need the capability.  
The mature move is not “no planning.” It is planning that serves empowered teams instead of replacing them.

**Why it works:** Extremely fair to SAFe practitioners. Slightly more “lesson” than “linger.”

### Ending C — A question to another VP

Close with the question you would ask a peer considering the same move:  
*Are you trying to escape a framework — or are you prepared to relocate ownership, instrumentation, and decision rights to the people closest to the customer problem?*

**Why it works:** Practical. Peer-level. Risk: can feel like a workshop ending if the question is too neat.

### Editorial recommendation

**End with Ending A**, and let Ending B’s dependency paradox appear in Act IV as the fair acknowledgment. Ending C can be a soft final line under A if it stays one sentence — not a bulleted takeaway block masquerading as an ending.

Do **not** end with the revenue chart. Revenue is context, not climax.

## 10. Counterarguments

Readers who still use SAFe successfully must feel seen. Intentionally acknowledge:

### Strongest arguments for staying with SAFe (or a heavyweight planning model)

1. **Dependency reality at true scale** — When dozens of teams share architecture and release trains, invisible dependencies are more expensive than visible ceremony. Cortney’s point is not nostalgia; it is systems thinking.
2. **Shared cadence creates organizational language** — ARTs, PI objectives, Inspect & Adapt give large groups a common clock. Without a replacement clock, organizations get calendar chaos and executive surprise.
3. **Coordination labor is real work** — Survey respondents noted project managers absorbed requirements, dependency discovery, and planning so builders could build. Removing that labor without redesigning it dumps invisible work onto engineers and PMs.
4. **Some “SAFe problems” are implementation problems** — Balaji’s notes themselves suggest the same SME/ownership failures can appear in Scrum/Kanban if practices and roles are weak. Fair essay must distinguish **framework** from **implementation**.
5. **Regulated / date-driven contexts** — Where external commitments and auditability dominate, centralized planning can be a feature. The essay should not imply every enterprise is ready for the same autonomy.

### Where the article should acknowledge strengths (placement)

| Strength | Place it |
| --- | --- |
| Dependency visibility | Act I (adoption) and Act IV (what you still need) |
| Cadence / common clock | Act I |
| Coordination labor | Act IV humility — what was underestimated |
| Implementation vs framework | Act II — short, crucial distinction |
| Not tossing planning entirely | Coda — “the question is what kind of planning” |

**Editorial rule:** At least one full paragraph in Act I and one in Act IV must be writable only by someone who is not trying to win an argument against SAFe.

## 14. Editorial Critique

Critique of the article **before it exists** — pressure-test the risk of the story we are about to tell.

### Where the argument is weakest

1. **Causation fog** — Throughput rose after the change. So did many other things (staffing, product mix, seasonality, leadership attention). If Draft 1 overclaims, sophisticated readers will discard the whole essay. Your “break even with SAFe” framing is the antidote — keep it central.
2. **Framework vs implementation** — Balaji already noted the same pains can appear without SAFe. If you blur that distinction, SAFe practitioners will correctly dismiss you as fighting a straw man.
3. **Outcome gap** — Your documents preach outcomes; your hardest numbers are still mostly output and sentiment. Act IV must confess this, not hide it. Confession increases trust; hiding invites the charge of hypocrisy.

### Where readers may disagree

- Leaders in highly regulated or heavily coupled platforms may argue you underweight dependency ceremony.
- Former PMO/project managers may feel the essay erases skilled coordination work.
- POM skeptics may say “empowered teams” is SVPG fashion with weak empirics.
- Some will say five months is too short to judge an operating model.

Anticipate these in the prose. Do not wait for comments.

### Where more evidence is needed

- One crisp **customer-facing** example of ownership change (even qualitative).
- A clearer **.NET 8 / safe-lite** vignette that is about decision rights, not personalities.
- Your personal turning-point scene (Tier 1 Q1) — without it, the essay risks reading like a well-sourced case memo instead of a leader’s reflection.
- Explicit statement of what you **kept** from SAFe-era planning discipline.

### Assumptions to challenge

- Assumption: “Centralized planning was the root problem.”  
  Challenge: Maybe architectural coupling and unclear product boundaries were the root, and SAFe was a coping mechanism. If true, the essay should say so — it makes Idea 1 stronger.
- Assumption: “Empowered teams are the destination.”  
  Challenge: Empowerment without instrumentation produces confident feature factories. Your survey already knows this; the companion article must carry the fix, but this essay must plant the warning.
- Assumption: “Everyone experienced SAFe the same way.”  
  Challenge: Some teams likely benefited from PI planning more than others. One sentence of heterogeneity prevents monoculture storytelling.

### How this becomes a five-year reference piece

Do these five things relentlessly:

1. **Make SAFe the case study, not the subject.** The subject is operating-model life cycles.
2. **Keep the incomplete middle.** Safe-lite is the insight competitors won’t publish.
3. **Be fair enough that a SAFe advocate quotes your Act I.**
4. **Prefer mechanisms over labels** (ownership, decision latency, dashboard theater, discovery/delivery split).
5. **Refuse the victory lap.** Timeless pieces end on a sharper question than they started with.

### Final editor’s challenge to you

The dossier could support a satisfying story: *We left SAFe, ownership improved, numbers went up.*

That story would get applause and be forgotten.

The harder story — and the one worthy of Building Better Software — is:

**We adopted a model that helped us scale. It worked until the way it worked began to prevent the next kind of scaling. We changed it. Parts got better. Then we discovered that frameworks are easy to remove and decision habits are not — and that the second discovery is the real work of product leadership.**

Write toward that. Cut anything that cannot survive that sentence.
