When SAFe Stops Scaling — Interview Answers
(Tier 1 questions from the Story Brief — answer in your own words, unpolished.)

1. The moment I concluded the model had stopped scaling. When the amount of time required to do work was oftentimes exceeded by the planning and coordination of that work. When we started tracking output and metrics and numbers without seeming to care about outcomes and customer focus. I remember on many occasions how frustrated I would be when it was so hard to get anything changed. Customers who pay us tens of millions of dollars would request something and we'd have to go to the SAFE committee (as a VP) to get approval and sometimes they'd simply say no. It would make me mad because we had no recourse. That group was too powerful and everyone felt SAFE was forced on us. When creativity became beholden to process and everything came to a grinding halt. When the PMO who ran SAFE became too powerful and out of touch with what we really do and that is to solve customer problems. We were a slave to the process. I remember on many occasions how pissed I'd get when the SAFE committee would meet and wouldn't even include product. It was really really frustration. 

2. What I originally believed SAFe would solve — and when that changed. I believed it would solve the complex dependencies that existed between our codebases and our legacy software. It changed when we started to get way too many status in the process, and not enough individual ownership over actual outcomes. It also caused a lot of personal frustration and caused silos between product and engineering which was not helpful. It got less personal which is a net loss in our software business. We deal with insurance claims, and that is a very personal thing when someone has a house fire or loses everything. My job is to help those people, they rely on me. It made me sad when I had to tell them I couldn't so something for months. They didn't understand or care about our process. This made me feel jaded about SAFe. Part of this it not SAFE's fault but the way we implemented it. The focus should be oh helping customers solve problems, not following some process internally. Safe became too important. Safe seemed to ensure we started a lot or work, but we had so much work in progress. 

3. The fairest paragraph I can write about SAFe. It is a framework, just like many others. It has some good qualities like how it handles prioritization, some of the ceremonies are valuable although I personally felt burdened by them and they were just to overbearing. (not all, there are too many) but some. The idea to push coordination between different groups is noble, but it forced too many communication points and too many people had to attend too many meetings. Developers were always in meetings, always context switching and that’s expensive and frustrating. Developers hated it, they felt like this was something forced on them. 

4. Coordination work I underestimated when scaffolding came down. We lost some of the formal meetings and ceremonies and we’ve had to add back a few. But that was more of a result of us learning the product operating model, which we continue to do. I feel much better about helping set strategy and helping the team understand what problems to solve, and then letting them solve those problems. That allows me to focus on my role and not waste my time in hours and days of meetings that sometimes just seemed like theatre. 

5. What made .NET 8 / “safe-lite” feel like the old model returning. Let’s exclude this from the research. 

6. Best proof that ownership moved for real. We are not where I want to be yet, but ownership moved away from the PMO which wasn’t even part of the product or engineering team, to the actual product and engineering teams. We aren’t great yet, and I'm not happy with where we are yet, but we are pushing responsibility down to the teams, but also autonomy to the smaller teams. This is our current focus. There seemed to be no ownership at all with SAFE. The PMO had no ownership and I felt like I was always defending and fighting for my product team because we had no control over outcomes, so I pushed back on taking responsibility as well. That is a bad bad place to be. I'd rather focus on extreme ownership, but without having the ability to control outcomes, that is a losing battle.

Fragments / observations
Safe moved product and engineering away from the product and those groups became beholden to the process. In the end, the process needs to be a means to the end, not the end itself. Customer outcome should always come first and not simply output. We are building solutions and experiences; this is not a factor where we churn out widgets. 

Things I Never Want To Forget
Every operating model eventually optimizes for something. Make sure it's the customer.
Customers don't care about our operating model, they just want their problems solved.
Processes exists to help solve customer problems. Full stop. 
The closer decisions are to customers, the better they become.
Ownership without authority creates learned helplessness.
Every company is different, so should be the processes. 
If you do promote individual ownership, you will eventually lose.
