# Scene Outline: When SAFe Stops Scaling

**Type:** Scene Outline (pre-draft)  
**Not:** article prose · traditional H2 outline · research expansion  
**Sources:** Story Brief + personal reflections (`thoughts.md`) + research dossier  
**Author exclusions honored:** no .NET 8 / “safe-lite” vignette · no survey-struggle deep dive  

**Central thesis (locked for rhythm):**  
Operating models do not fail because they are wrong. They stop scaling when the problems they were designed to solve give way to problems their structure now creates — and leadership keeps optimizing the old solution.

**Cuts made for inevitability:**  
revenue-as-proof · AI subplot · role matrix · portfolio-governance mechanics · safe-lite relapse scene · survey lows chart · org-chart tourism · private score-settling messages  

**Rhythm:** 8 scenes. If it doesn’t advance the thesis, it isn’t here.

---

## Scene 1 — The No

**Purpose**  
Put the reader in the moment the model stopped being abstract.

**Emotional tone**  
Controlled anger · disbelief · duty to customers colliding with process

**Key idea**  
When planning and coordination routinely cost more than the work — and decision rights sit farther from the customer than the people who serve them — the model has inverted.

**Supporting evidence**  
- Planning/coordination time exceeding doing the work  
- Output and metrics tracked harder than outcomes  
- Process becoming the thing the organization optimized  

**Personal story**  
VP of Product. Customer paying tens of millions requests something reasonable. Path runs through a SAFe committee. Sometimes the answer is simply no. No real recourse. Product not always in the room. PMO running the framework felt more powerful than the people accountable for customer problems.

**Transition → Scene 2**  
Before judging the framework, show why a serious organization reached for it in the first place.

---

## Scene 2 — Why We Reached for It

**Purpose**  
Earn trust from readers who still use scaling frameworks well. Kill the hit-piece reading.

**Emotional tone**  
Respectful · clear-eyed · slightly nostalgic for the clarity it once offered

**Key idea**  
Idea 1 — Useful until it isn’t. Shared cadence, dependency visibility, and prioritization discipline are real strengths at a certain stage of scale.

**Supporting evidence**  
- 2022 serious implementation (ARTs, PI planning, program boards — not casual experimentation)  
- Structural need: many products, many teams, complex codebase/legacy dependencies  
- Cortney: dependency mapping/scheduling was one of the few great things SAFe provided  
- Fair internal view: prioritization mechanics and some ceremonies had value; coordination across groups is a noble aim  
- Survey/comment residue: project managers once absorbed heavy coordination so builders could build  

**Personal story**  
Original belief: SAFe would help manage complex dependencies between codebases and legacy software. That belief was sincere.

**Transition → Scene 3**  
The question was never whether planning had value. It was what kind of planning — and who the planning served.

---

## Scene 3 — Naming the Inversion

**Purpose**  
Give the reader the intellectual hinge: the model begins optimizing itself.

**Emotional tone**  
Unease · recognition · forensic calm (not rant)

**Key idea**  
Idea 2 — When fixed-scope, fixed-timeline planning wraps itself around “Agile,” discovery loses to commitment theater.

**Supporting evidence**  
- March 2023 Program CI: PI planning as “a mini-waterfall wrapper on Agile”  
- Difficulty changing priorities inside the increment  
- Commitments outrunning readiness / late discovery of dependencies  
- Balaji cluster (compressed, not a laundry list): late SME involvement · dependencies found after backlog entry · missing end-to-end ownership · dashboards that didn’t match unplanned work  

**Personal story**  
None required as lead. Author presence = we saw this from inside while still operating in the model — not as outside critics.

**Transition → Scene 4**  
Once named, show what the inversion felt like in the body of the work — tools, meetings, silos, customers waiting.

---

## Scene 4 — The Cost of Becoming Process

**Purpose**  
Make Idea 2 visceral without becoming a complaint catalog.

**Emotional tone**  
Weariness · sadness under the frustration · protectiveness toward builders and customers

**Key idea**  
Process exists to help solve customer problems. When process becomes the end, people and customers pay interest.

**Supporting evidence**  
- Ceremony load: multi-day PI planning; developers in too many meetings; expensive context switching  
- Tooling as archive of confusion: nearly 40 Jira statuses on one product workflow  
- Product–Engineering distance / silo formation under heavyweight coordination  
- Distinction held lightly: some of this is framework; some is implementation — both mattered  

**Personal story**  
Insurance claims work is personal — house fires, total losses. Having to tell customers they would wait months because of an internal process felt wrong. Customers don’t care about the operating model. They want the problem solved. That gap created jadedness — and clarity.

**Transition → Scene 5**  
The deepest damage wasn’t meeting count. It was what happened to ownership.

---

## Scene 5 — Ownership Without Authority

**Purpose**  
Deliver the essay’s moral engine — the line readers will underline.

**Emotional tone**  
Hard-won · sober · no self-pity

**Key idea**  
Ownership without authority creates learned helplessness. If you cannot influence outcomes, “extreme ownership” becomes a losing battle — and leaders start defending instead of deciding.

**Supporting evidence**  
- End-to-end ownership missing across mid-level managers, PMs, and product teams  
- Features stalled because one or two stories remained incomplete while “ownership” was everywhere and nowhere  
- PMO held coordination power without product/engineering skin in the outcome  
- Work-in-progress swelling: the system was good at starting work, weaker at finishing customer value  

**Personal story**  
Felt like constantly defending and fighting for the product team with little control over outcomes — so responsibility itself became something to push back on. That is a bad place for a product organization to live.

**Transition → Scene 6**  
By then the organization wasn’t really arguing about a framework brand. It was arguing about who was allowed to own a customer problem.

---

## Scene 6 — Moving the Center of Gravity

**Purpose**  
Show the change as structural relocation of decision rights — not “we cancelled a ceremony.”

**Emotional tone**  
Determined · practical · still unfinished by design

**Key idea**  
Leaving a heavyweight model only matters if ownership moves to durable product and engineering teams closest to the work.

**Supporting evidence**  
- October 2024: product management reorganized around product domains; explicit turn toward product operating model principles  
- Intent: faster decisions, stronger customer alignment, discovery and delivery owned together — not thrown over the fence  
- Public hinge in the org: people already asking whether PI planning would continue  

**Personal story**  
Relief at reclaiming time for strategy and problem definition — letting teams solve — instead of days of meetings that sometimes felt like theater. Also: honesty that some formal coordination had to be added back later while learning the new model.

**Transition → Scene 7**  
Leaders will ask: did anything actually get better? Answer without a victory lap.

---

## Scene 7 — Break Even First

**Purpose**  
Prove seriousness about evidence. Keep causation humble.

**Emotional tone**  
Measured · slightly proud · intellectually careful

**Key idea**  
The first honest bar was not “transformation success.” It was: can we at least break even with the old model on delivery — then see whether ownership actually moved.

**Supporting evidence**  
- Explicit break-even framing in the March 2025 SAFe vs POM comparison  
- Estimate Pillar window: epics 27→30 · stories 247→301 · independent/ad hoc stories 126→106 (interpret as focus, labeled as interpretation)  
- Caption discipline: adjacent periods, same pillar, not a controlled experiment  
- Later leadership note: clearer ownership at team level; many prior SAFe-era issues reduced; next hard problem = aligning longer-horizon objectives across teams  

**Personal story**  
Not where I want to be yet. Ownership moved away from a PMO that wasn’t part of product or engineering — toward the actual teams. That direction is right. Completion is not.

**Transition → Scene 8**  
Early improvement is dangerous if it convinces leaders the hard part is over.

---

## Scene 8 — Still Becoming

**Purpose**  
The lasting contribution. Refuse the tidy ending. Seed the companion essay without writing it.

**Emotional tone**  
Honest · slightly chastened · useful · forward-leaning without brochure energy

**Key idea**  
Changing the operating model is easier than finishing the relocation of ownership, autonomy, and customer focus. Frameworks come off faster than habits mature.

**Supporting evidence**  
- Had to restore a few coordination rhythms after removing heavy ceremony — learning, not relapse-as-scandal  
- Dependency paradox: what SAFe did well (visibility/scheduling) still needed in lighter form  
- Current focus: push responsibility and autonomy further down to smaller teams  
- Companion boundary: outcome instrumentation, discovery cadence, PM craft — next essay, not this one  

**Personal story**  
Better now at setting strategy and clarifying problems to solve — then getting out of the way. Still unfinished. Prefer extreme ownership only when authority matches it.

**Transition → Coda**  
Widen the lens: this was never only about one framework’s brand name.

---

## Coda — What Every Model Eventually Optimizes For

**Purpose**  
Leave the reader with a transferable diagnostic — not a summary, not a toolkit dump.

**Emotional tone**  
Quiet authority · peer to peer · lingering

**Key idea**  
Thesis landing: every operating model eventually optimizes for something. Make sure it’s the customer. Strengths become constraints when leadership keeps optimizing the old solution.

**Supporting evidence (warning signs — sparse, not a checklist article)**  
- Coordination routinely costs more than creation  
- Dashboards look healthier than customer reality  
- Decisions require a committee farther from the work than the people who will live with the outcome  
- Ownership is demanded without authority  
- Creativity waits on process  

**Personal story**  
None new. Echo only: customers don’t care about the operating model.

**Final beat (one line to land on in Draft 1 — not written as prose here)**  
Question for another VP: Are you trying to escape a framework — or are you prepared to put decision rights next to the customer problem?

**No Scene 9.** Stop here.
